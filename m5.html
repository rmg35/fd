not an immediate success

  • late 1800s - early 1900s, American meat industry was sus, especially ground meat
  • ppl thought ground meat was a way to hide inferior / rotten meat
  • Embalmed Beef Scandal:
    • Spanish-American War, 1898
    • US poorly equipped to feed its soldiers
    • Secretary of War, Russel Alger, commissioned Chicago's big 3 to provide meat for the army at the lowest possible cost
    • army urged him to use local cattle in Cuba, but he wanted to support the new and upcoming industry
    • meat packers took advantage and took major shortcuts, using bad meat with untested preservatives
    • hundreds of tons of rotten meat arrived in cuba
    • 2x soldiers died from food poisoning than combat
  • 1906: Upton Sinclair
    • socialist, published The Jungle
    • described exploitation and harsh working conditions of immigrants
    • many immigrants worked in meat packing industry
    • rats in the meat, no hand-washing, washed their hands in the water used for ladling the sausage
    • all sorts of shit made it to market
    • some jobs rarely got done, like cleaning the waste barrels
    • much of the waste was sold to production
    • T. Roosevelt was pissed, and demanded congress take action
  • congress passed laws, and the meat industry cleaned up its act, but american trust wouldn't be regained for a time

white castle

  • WWI raging in Europe
  • immigrant population in US exploding
  • many worked in factories and were exploited by their employers
  • poor, lived in awful conditions
  • unsanitary diners, "Greasy Spoons", which often served burgers
    • burgers still had a bad rep at this time
  • 1921, Kansas, Walt Anderson: sought to overturn America's distrust in burgers
  • opened a burger shop in Wichita with the kitchen in plain view
  • employees wore clean uniforms
  • called the restaurant White Castle
  • burgers were actually meatballs, which later got flattened and put between dinner rolls
  • tiny burgers were a huge success, especially among factory workers
  • he tried opening a second location in a dentist, but they wanted to turn him down, because ew immigrants gross
  • real estate agent, Billy Ingram, decided to partner with Anderson
  • Ingram began acquiring places for expansion
  • spread across the mid-west
  • Ingram assumed control of the business, to Anderson's dismay
  • he demanded perfection from his employees, so much so that he closed all locations for two weeks to intensely train everyone on making consistent burgers
  • WC expanded throughout the Great Depression, and even began the practice of putting coupons in the paper
  • Anderson had enough though, and flipped his share to Ingram in 1933
  • Ingram moved headquarters to Columbus, OH and continued expansion
  • relatively small by today's fast food standards
  • first fast-food and hamburger chain
  • no franchise locations, all are family-owned

krystal

  • 1932: Rody Davenport Jr. and J. Glenn Sherrill
  • Chattanooga, TN
  • spotless restaurant, good service, low price
  • Davenport basically copied a White Castle he saw in Chicago
  • original building was manufactured in Chicago and shipped to Chattanooga
  • Krystal Kreed: clean establishment, best food, properly cooked, quick, efficient, and courteous service, at the lowest price
  • no takeout, inexpensive dishes
  • originally no franchises, but Davenport caved
  • several employees filed a class action lawsuit over altered time cards
  • this led to Krystal filing for bankruptcy, which in turn led to them being sold to Port Royal Holdings
  • they're still not doing great!
  • oldest hamburger chain in the south
  • named Krystal in reference to its cleanliness
