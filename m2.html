6 - Damned spoiled food

  • food preservation targets slowing the rate at which food spoils

    • spoilage can be caused by:
      • microorganisms (bacteria, mold)
      • oxidation (rancidity)
      • insects
      • etc
    • cooking is a means of preserving food, at least temporarily
    • one of the earliest methods: drying
      • preserves food by removing moisture, slowing microbial spoilage
      • 1000BC, chinese using salt and smoking to preserve foods
      • later, fermentation, which also adds new tastes
      • most significant changes have come via recent discoveries (and wars)

  • the ancient chinese discovered fermentation as a means of preservation

    • originally vegetables, meat was added later

  • salting also existed

    • used in ancient egypt to preserve mummies
    • later realized its use in preserving food
    • drying and smoking date back to 6000 BC
      • smoking also adds flavor

  • early preservation in egypt targeted grains and cereals

    • primarily employed drying and storage in rudimentary silos
    • crop production relied on the nile flooding, so these silos provided them backup if it ever didn't
    • drying provided jobs thought to be distinguished and important

  • fermentation of grains lead to beer

    • produced as far back as 4000BC by the egyptians
    • early on, not really alcoholic
    • wine fermentation came later, and was way more complex
      • reserved for the elite
    • ancient egyptians also enjoyed drying and salting fish and meat

  • earliest evidence of wine making dates back to 6000BC

    • grape growing spread across egypt
    • writing was first developed in this region in the late 4th century BC
      • evidence of ration lists, food distribution records, inventories of granaries
      • ancient preservation methods archived

  • tannur ovens were primitive ovens used for cooking grains and breads in mesopotamia

    • made of clay and gypsum

    • capable of withstanding 1500F

    • mesopotamians learned to press the dried fish to release oils

      • oils used for cooking, flavoring, and preserving foods

    • early beer was low in alcohol, but provided proteins and nutrients to the egyptian diet

    • african mummies have been discovered with high levels of tatracycline, a modern antibiodic

      • it's because of the soil getting into the fermentation process

    • ancient egyptians knew how to use alcohol to treat infections

    • in the ruins of pompeii, archelogists have found jars of fruit preserved in honey

      • low moisture content of the honey would've prevented growth of microorganisms / protected from oxidation
      • rome is the source of the first preserved ham
        • like modern day PROSCIUTTO
      • vikings laid fish on their ships to let the wind and salt water dry them
      • early native americans made a product called pemmican
        • remove fat from meat
        • cook meat
        • fat and meat into a paste
        • add dried berries and spices
        • molded into squares with low moisture content
        • basicall native american energy bars
        • important to fur traders in 17th - 19th centuries

  • fire was the first revolution of food, preservation was the second

    • Nicolas Appert, french chef, confectioner, distiller
    • napoleon needed a way to get food to his soldiers
    • traditional food preservation measures weren't cutting it
    • prize of 12,000 francs to anyone who can effectively preserve food

  • appert took the challenge and discovered canning

    • canning was popular in WWI & II due to rationing

  • appert knew excluding air was critical to preserve food

    • strong corks that supported the inside and outside of wide-mouth bottles and withstood pressure
    • glue out of isinglass (dried swim bladders of fish) to join pieces of cork to fit the jars
    • mixed quicklime (cement) and water to seal corks to the tops of bottles
    • wide mouths of bottles == wide variety of food could fit

  • appert cooked various foods and them put them into jars

    • jars went into large pots of water and were boiled

  • appert spent 7 years to perfect his work

    • samples sent to napoleon's army pleased napoleon
    • appert awarded the 12,000 franc prize
    • canning sometimes referred to as appertization
    • institute of food technologists presents a Nicolas Appert award
    • "Nicolas Appert Year" in france, 2010

  • appert's method spread like wildfire

    • peter durand invented the tin can (actually iron)
    • john hall and byran dorking (EN) opened the first canning factory in 1813
    • 1846, henry evans (US) invented a better machine for making the cans
    • 1858, john mason invented the "mason" jar, which lead to home canning
    • frank and edmund ball began producing "ball" jars after mason's patent expired

  • canning was important, as was freezing

  • 1000BC, chinese freezed foods outside of the winter

    • dug a large cellar in the ground and line the walls in ice
    • greeks and romans had similar tactics, also stored under cool water
    • extended shelf-life, but didn't provide long-term preservation
    • ancient incas learned higher altitude == longer lasting food
      • they were basically freeze drying their foods

  • freeze drying food

    • by reducing pressure, water would directly vaporize from foods
    • developed during WWII to preserve blood plasma and penicillin
      • penicillin was discovered in 1928 by Alexander Fleming
    • henry nestle used freeze drying to preserve coffee
    • james mercer pioneered freeze drying coffee, somehow

  • clarence birdseye, "father of frozen food"

    • he didn't invent freezing food, but he drastically improved it
    • became an outdoorsman selling trapped animals for fur or research
    • had to drop out when money was tight, worked for the US Department of Agriculture's Biological Survey
    • howard t. ricketts discovered the bacteria that causes Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever: Rickettsia
    • anyway, birdseye quit his job and went hunting for furs on dogsled
    • dude ate almost anything
    • married, had a kid, moved to the middle of nowhere
    • noticed natives freezing food, and how it was fresh once thawed
    • began experimenting with frozen fish
    • two major innovations:
      1. importance of rapid freezing for best quality
      2. reail consumer packaging
    • no one trusted his food, so he went bankrupt
    • new company: now we're inventing freezers
    • went down a belt and got super cold somehow idfc
    • first commercially practical freezer
    • sold his shares in the company to Charles Post / Postum Cereal Company
    • stuck around to do research
    • still unsuccessful, so birdeye worked on display cases
    • WWII saw japan taking over where tin gets made, so we needed something new to put shit in
    • grocers filled empty space with frozen foods

  • expiration dates

    • highest quality until this date
    • you don't have to throw shit away immediately after the expiration date unless it's something super parishable
    • rule of thumb: if you don't see anything wrong, and don't smell anything wrong, there's probably nothing wrong
