10 - How sweet it is

sugar

  • 10,000BC

    • honey from wild bees
    • bees domesticated ~9000BC
    • early domestic beehives
      • hollow logs
      • had to be destroyed to get honey
      • reusable beehives weren't used until ~2000 years ago
    • honey: 40% fructose, 35% glucose, 9% other shit
    • honey is sweeter than sugar (64 cal vs. 16 cal)

  • sugarcane in new guinea

    • natives chewed on it for its sweetness
    • learned to cultivate it
    • 2000 years, made its way to philippines and india
    • india first to refine sugar (records from ~100AD)
    • 327BC, greeks and romans got sugar from india
    • Nearchus, officer in alexander the great's army: "reed in inda that brings forth honey without the help of bees, from which an intoxicating drink is made, [...] plant bears no fruit."
    • ~650, sugarcane is in arabia
      • arabs mastered growing, refining, cooking
      • sugar considered medicinal and a wealthy delicacy
      • sugar + ground almonds -> mazipan (sweet paste)
    • crusades of 1099 -> europeans in jerusalem
      • they now have sugar
      • sugar remained rare and expensive until 1300s
      • 1400s & 1500s, spanish colonize canary island off coast of NW africa
      • sugar plantations worked by enslaved indigenous people
      • 1493, columbus brought sugarcane to hispanola, which became the most important sugar producer in the new world
      • late 1500s: sugar plantations spread throughout brazil
        • african slave trade increases
        • 1600s: coffee, tea, and chocolate in hands of europe
        • further increase for african slave sugar

  • sugar's medical properties

    • Tabernaemontanus, german guy, said some shit

  • sugar = sucrose = glucose and fructose

    • all green plants produce sugar
    • not all plants are equal in sugar production
    • most sugar comes from sugarcane or sugar beets
    • cane: 14%, beets: 16%

  • sugar production

    • extracted from plants
      • amber in color cuz of molasses
    • centrifugation removes molasses
    • remaining crystals are shipped
    • molasses added back for brown sugar
    • excess molasses used by feed companies, bakeries, distillers, pharmaceutical companies
    • fibrous waste from sugarcane processing is used as a biofuel for heat and electricity
    • also pulp for building materials
    • residue from sugar beet processing used in animal feed

  • sugar originally luxurious, for elite class

    • sugar and slaves in the british colonies kept them funded
    • Etienne de Bore spent life savings on sugarcane crop
      • made a huge profit off the backs of slaves
    • mid 1800s: louisiana producing 1/4 of the world's cane sugar

      • 125k slaves

    • work was back-breaking and dangerous
    • cane harvested, carried to carts
    • cane stalks are heavy -> backbreaking work
      • heat and humitiy of louisianna
    • refineries were dangerous
      • workers constantly exposed to boiling kettles, open furnaces, exposed grinding rollers
    • children were not spared

  • us produces 9mil tons of sugar /yr

    • 11k farmers on 2mil acres of land
    • Domino Sugar: biggest players
    • sugarcane is louisiana's cash crop

  • two categories of sugar

    • natural sugar, and added sugar
    • avg. american consumes ~60lbs of sugar /yr
    • kids eat more

    • 2x recommeneded amt of added sugar consumed /day on avg (per American Heart Association)

    • main culprit: sugary drinks, snacks and sweets

  • too much sugar

    • difficulty concentrating
      • surge in energy -> crash
      • too many cycles caused a brain fog effect
    • cravings
      • end of the cycle -> NEED MORE
    • weight gain
      • ~300 cal/day on avg, which is 100cal more than recommended
    • type 2 diabetes
      • leads to obesity, high blood sugar, high blood pressure, atherosclerosis (hardening of arteries)
        • all of that leads to heart disease (no 1 cause of US death)
    • difficulty sleeping
    • tooth decay
      • bacteria in mouth convert sugar to acids, acids erode teeth

  • sugar by any other name is sugar

    • 2021: FDA requires all manufacturers to specify amt of added sugars

  • added sugars

    • fruit juice concentrate
      • just as bad as straight sugar
      • manufacturers used it to get around having to say there's sugar in smth
      • people might've thought it was healthier
    • high fructose corn syrup
      • added to many foods, especially soft drinks
      • 120x - 160x sweeter than sugar
      • cheap, used to achieve the same sweetness for less

  • HFCS

    • corn starch + a-amylase = long chains of glucose (oligosaccharides.)
    • treated with the enzyme glucoamlyase, hydrolyzes oligo into glucose monosaccharides
    • glucose treated with glucose isomerase -> 42% fructose, 50% glucose
    • mixtures goes through a process of separation and enrichment -> 90% fructose
    • blended into high froctose corn syrup
    • HFCS discovered in '57, didn't take off until trade restrictions led to a sugar shortage
    • plenty of corn, so we used it on HFCS
    • 1980: coke quietly switched to HFCS
    • HFCS isn't all turbo evil or whatever
    • some states imposing a "fat tax" on sugary drinks

  • Saccharin

    • Ira Remsen, researcher comissioned to develop testing of sugar for adulterants
    • working with sulfobenzoic acid (coal tar)
    • Constantin Fahlberg, russian chemist who came to work with remsen
    • fahlberg working with benzoic sulfimide (other coal tar), got some on his hands -> ate a roll and noticed it was sweet
    • taste tested all of his chemicals -> it was benzoic sulfimide
    • patented that shit as saccharin
    • criticized for being hazardous
      • Dr. Harvey Wiley wanted it banned
      • President T. Roosevelt wasn't having it tho, so wiley resigned
      • later called an adulterant, but then sugar ran out in WWI
      • later later, prohibited from adding anything to food that had any potential to cause cancer
      • Cumberland Packing Corp. becan selling powdered saccharin as Sweet 'n Low, and framing it as a weight loss product
        • popular with dieters and diabetics
      • studies shows saccharin giving rats bladder cancer
        • violated above Delaney Clause
      • activists fought its banning though, so congress humored them
      • considered a carcinogen until '97 when we found humans metabolize saccharin differently to rats
        • after this, you didn't have to put a warning label on products using it anymore

  • aspartame

    • NutraSweet and Equal
    • 16 cal/tsp, but 200x the sweetening power
    • bitter flavor
    • aspartic acid + methyl ester of phenylalanine
    • normally, humans can fully metabolize this stuff
      • ppl with phenylketonuria can't metabolize the phenyl.
      • intellectual disabilities, brain dmg, seizures
      • foods have to contain a warning for this shit

  • cyclamate

    • low cal.
    • 30x sweeter than sugar
    • not approved in US and england
    • controversial animal study reported high doses of cyclamates led to tumors in lab animals
      • thus banned in US
    • TaB, first diet cola beverage, was hit hard
    • many soft drink manufacturers ceased
    • FDA currently considering re-approval

  • acesulfame potassium

    • acesulfame K
    • 200x sweeter than sugar
    • non-nutritive (0 cal)
    • heat stable; popular in baked goods

  • sucralose

    • similar to sugar
      • made by treating sugar with multistep chemical process
      • non-digestible by humans -> no calories
    • 600x sweeter than sugar
    • sold as Splenda

  • rebiana

    • highly pure extractof rabaudioside-A
    • common name is Stevia, discovered by Moises Santiago Bertoni
    • 200x sweeter, 0 cal
    • FDA called it sage in '08
    • coke and cargill jointly developed their brand Truvia

  • neotame

    • 2nd newest sweetener in the US
    • NutraSweet Co. sold as Newtame
    • similar to aspartame
    • 7k - 13k times sweeter
    • 0 cal
    • clean, sugar-like taste

  • advantame

    • newest
    • Ajinomoto
    • analog of aspartame
    • 20k times sweeter
    • 0cal, no off flavors
